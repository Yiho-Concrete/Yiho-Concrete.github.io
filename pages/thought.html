    • Perspective and Simplicity

    A description of a phenomenon is defined by the perspective.

    A person sitting in front of you can be both static and dynamic. The person is static in relative to you and the person is dynamic in relative to an astronaut who is observing you from the space.

    Through intensive reading, one is able to perceive a phenomenon from different perspectives. However, when presenting, one is often ambitiously keen on showing the whole picture. It is a good intention but difficult to achieve in practice. The presented work is sometimes merely a collection of jigsaws that consists of overwhelming and discrete information. One shows and tells what is observed, which is pure information; a step away from being knowledge.

    To form a knowledge, one collects the discrete information scattered around, piles one piece on the top of another, glue some pieces together, or break down some to smaller ones. It is important to take out the unnecessary branches to reveal the essence of the knowledge tree. As the sculptor, Constantin Brancusi, once said, "in art, one does not aim for simplicity, one achieves it unintentionally as one gets closer to the real meaning of things." To some extent, writing a literature review is also an art.
